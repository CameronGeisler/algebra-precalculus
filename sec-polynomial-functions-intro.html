    Section 3.1 Polynomial Functions Intro

    Recall that previously, we considered constant functions, linear functions, and quadratic functions,
    🔗
    • \(f(x) = b\text{,}\) whose graph is a horizontal line.
      🔗
    • \(f(x) = mx + b\text{,}\) whose graph is a (diagonal) line.
      🔗
    • \(f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c\text{,}\) whose graph is a parabola.
      🔗
    It turns out that we can also consider functions that are made up of higher powers of \(x\text{,}\) like \(x^3, x^4, x^5\text{,}\) and so on.
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.1 Power Functions

    • Consider \(y = x\text{,}\) \(y = x^2\text{,}\) \(y = x^3\text{,}\) \(y = x^4\text{,}\) \(y = x^5\text{,}\) \(y = x^6\text{,}\) etc. Observe that the function with even integer powers have a ``U-shape" whereas the odd integer powers have an ``S-shape".
      🔗
    • Consider \(y = -x\text{,}\) \(y = -x^2\text{,}\) \(y = -x^3\text{,}\) \(y = -x^4\text{,}\) \(y = -x^5\text{.}\) These are a reflection over the \(x\)-axis of the previous graphs.
      🔗
    • Then, consider adding terms of lower degree. Observe that the rough of the graph is determined by the highest power of \(x\) (called the degree of the polynomial).
      🔗
    • Consider \(y = 3x + 1\) and \(y = -2x -1\text{,}\) \(y = 3x^3 - x^2 + 3\text{,}\) \(y = -4x^3 + x + 1\text{.}\) The rough shape of the graph is determined by the sign of the number in front of the highest power of \(x\) (called the leading coefficient).
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.2 Polynomial Functions

    Basically, a polynomial function is a function made of up powers of \(x\text{,}\) combined with numbers through multiplication, addition, and subtraction. Sometimes, we refer to them simply as polynomials.
    🔗

    Definition 3.1.1. Polynomial Function.

    A polynomial function is a function of the form,
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = a_n x^n + a_{n-1} x^{n-1} + \dots + a_2 x^2 + a_1 x + a_0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    where \(a_n, a_{n-1}, \dots, a_2, a_1, a_0 \in \mathbb{R}\) are numbers, and \(a_n \neq 0\text{.}\) The coefficients of a polynomial function, \(a_k\) are given using subscripted letters. In general, \(a_k\) represents the coefficient of the term \(x^k\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • The degree of a polynomial function is the exponent on its highest power of \(x\text{.}\) Here, this is a polynomial of degree \(n\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • The term with the highest power of \(x\text{,}\) \(a_n x^n\text{,}\) is called the leading term, and its coefficient \(a_n\) is called the leading coefficient.
      🔗
    🔗
    • A constant function \(f(x) = k\) is a polynomial function of degree 0.
      🔗
    • A linear function, \(f(x) = mx + b\) (where \(m \neq 0\)), is a polynomial function of degree 1.
      🔗
    • A quadratic function \(f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c\) (where \(a \neq 0\)), is a polynomial function of degree 2.
      🔗
    Roughly, the higher the degree, the more complicated the polynomial is.
    🔗
    The domain of a polynomial is all real numbers, or \((-\infty,\infty)\text{,}\) because it only requires addition, subtraction, and multiplication of numbers, and these operations are always defined.
    🔗

    Example 3.1.2. Example.

    The expression \(4x^3 - 3.6x^2 - \sqrt{2}\) is a polynomial of degree 3.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.1.3. Example.

    The expression \(x^{3/2} - x^4 + 1\) is not a polynomial, as \(3/2\) is not a non-negative integer.
    🔗
    🔗
    Polynomials of degree 3 are sometimes called \textbf{cubic}, degree 4 is \textbf{quartic}, degree 5 is \textbf{quintic}, and degree 6 is \textbf{sextic} or \textbf{hexic}.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.3 Graph of a Polynomial Function

    The graph of a polynomial function is both \textbf{smooth} (in that it contains no sharp corners) and \textbf{continuous} (it has no ``breaks" and can be drawn without lifting your pencil off the page).
    🔗
    A polynomial has possibly multiple points where it turns around, going from increasing to decreasing, or vice versa. It turns out that a polynomial of degree \(n\) has at most \(n - 1\) turning points.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.4 End Behavior of Polynomial Functions

    As we’ve seen, sometimes polynomial functions go right and up, and sometimes they go right and down, and sometimes left and up and other times left and down. Whether a polynomial goes up or down on the right or left is what we call its \textit{end behavior}.
    🔗

    Definition 3.1.4. End Behavior.

    The \textbf{end behavior} of a function is its behavior as \(x\) becomes large in either the positive or negative direction. In other words, as \(\abs{x}\text{,}\) or the magnitude of \(x\text{,}\) becomes large.
    🔗
    🔗
    Graphically, end behavior is the behavior to the ``far right" or ``far left" side of the graph. The graph of a polynomial function may increase and decrease on different intervals, however it will ``eventually" tend towards some behavior.
    🔗
    To describe end behavior, we often use a notation called \textbf{arrow notation},
    🔗
    • \(x \to \infty\) can be read as ``$x$ goes to infinity".
      🔗
    • \(x \to -\infty\) is ``$x$ goes to negative infinity".
      🔗
    • If the polynomial \(f\) increases without bound (goes up and up), we write \(f(x) \to \infty\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • If \(f\) decreases without bound (goes down and down), we write \(f(x) \to -\infty\text{.}\)
      🔗
    Observe that the end behavior of a polynomial function is determined by the leading term. This is intuitively because when \(x\) is large (either large positive or large negative), the leading term dominates the other terms, in the sense that it’s size is much larger than the others, to where the others are insignificant.
    🔗
    This can be summarized in the \textit{leading coefficient test}.

    Subsection 3.1.6 x-Intercepts of a Polynomial Function

    The \(x\)-intercept(s) of a polynomial function are the solutions to the equation \(f(x) = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 3.1.7. Zero (Root) of a Function.

    A number \(c\) is a \textbf{zero} or (\textbf{root}) of \(f(x)\) if \(f(c) = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • The number \(c\) is a zero if and only if \(c\) is an \(x\)-intercept of the graph of \(f(x)\text{.}\)
      🔗
    🔗
    We will explore various techniques in order to solve an equation like this. In general, solving this equation by hand and exactly is very difficult, and sometimes impossible.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.7 Remark

    Technically, the zero polynomial \(f(x) = 0\) is said to have degree \(-\infty\text{,}\) not 0 (for technical reasons that will become clear later).
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.8 Degree of a Product of Polynomials

    Theorem 3.1.8. Degree of a Product.

    The degree of a product of polynomials is the sum of the degrees. In particular, the product of \(n\) linear factors is a polynomial of degree \(n\text{.}\)
