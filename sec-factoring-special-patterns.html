    Section 2.3 Factoring Special Patterns

    Subsection 2.3.1 Difference of Squares

    Example 2.3.1. Motivation.

    When multiplying two binomials that are almost the same, except for one having a positive sign and one having a negative, there is a special pattern. Observe the following expansions,
    🔗
    \begin{align*} (x-2)(x+2) \amp = x^2 + 2x - 2x - 4\\ \amp = x^2 - 4 \end{align*}
    Notice that the middle terms \(2x\) and \(-2x\) cancel out.
    🔗
    \begin{align*} (x-6)(x+6) \amp = x^2 + 6x - 6x - 36\\ \amp = x^2 - 36\\ (x+10)(x-10) \amp = x^2 + 10x - 10x - 100\\ \amp = x^2 - 100 \end{align*}
    In general, when expanding an expression of the form \((x-a)(x+a)\) (where the two numbers are opposites of each other), the middle two terms always cancel out, because they are opposites of each other. The result is \(x\) squared, minus the number \(a\) squared.
    🔗
    🔗
    In general,
    🔗
    \begin{gather*} (a+b)(a-b) = a^2 - b^2 \end{gather*}
    This identity for expanding can be thought of in reverse, as a identity for factoring,
    🔗
    \begin{gather*} \boxed{a^2 - b^2 = (a-b)(a+b)} \end{gather*}
    In other words, for an expression of the form \(a^2 - b^2\) (where there are two things being squared, and they’re subtracted), it can be factored as \((a-b)(a+b)\text{.}\) It’s called difference of squares, “difference” because of the minus sign, and “squares” because both terms are perfect squares.
    🔗

    🔗
    1. There are two terms.

      🔗
      🔗
    3. Each term is a perfect square.
      🔗
      🔗
    If an expression has only 2 terms, then this is a good indicator that it could be factored using difference of squares.
