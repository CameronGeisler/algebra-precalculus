    Section 9.1 Radian Measure

    There is another unit for measuring angles, called radian, which turns out to be more useful than degrees, particularly in calculus.
    üîó

    Definition 9.1.1.

    Radians are a unit of angles such that,
    üîó
    \begin{gather*} \boxed{\pi \text{ radians} = 180^{\circ}} \qquad \text{or, equivalently,} \qquad \boxed{2\pi \text{ radians} = 360^{\circ}} \end{gather*}
    üîó
    In other words,
    üîó
    • \(\pi\) radians is a half-rotation.
      üîó
      üîó
    • \(2\pi\) radians is a full rotation.
      üîó
      üîó
    • The symbol for radians is ‚Äúrad‚Äù, however the symbol is often omitted. So, if an angle has no unit, it should be assumed to be radians (and for degrees, use \(^\circ\)).
      üîó
      üîó
    The equivalence \(\pi = 180^{\circ}\) can be thought of as a conversion factor, a ratio to convert between these two angle units (analogous to how 1 inch = 2.54 cm, or how 1 hour = 60 minutes).
    üîó

    Subsection 9.1.3 Motivating Radians

    Here, we will step back and explore the intuition behind radians, and how they come about to measure angles.
    üîó

    Example 9.1.7. Alternate Angular Units.

    First, it is helpful to step back and understand the idea of how angles can be measured.
    üîó
    Degrees are a unit of rotation such that 360 degrees make a full rotation. They are useful practical applications (historically, for navigation, architecture, and astronomy), because you can divide 360 evenly into many numbers (like 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 9, 10, 12, etc.), so many common angles have whole number values (like \(30^{\circ}, 45^{\circ}, 60^{\circ}, 90^{\circ}\text{,}\) etc.).
    üîó
    However, if we were starting from scratch, with no prior knowledge of degrees, we could consider other useful units of measuring angles. For example:
    üîó
    • The gradian, where a quarter rotation is defined to be 100 gradians. Then, a full rotation would be 400 gradians.
      üîó
      üîó
    • Or, 1 turn could be defined as a full rotation. Then, a half rotation is \(1/2\) turns, or a quarter rotation is \(1/4\) turns.
      üîó
      üîó
    • For units inspired by the fact that a pizza has 8 slices, a unit could be called a slice, defined to be \(1/8\)th of a full rotation. Then, 8 slices would be a full rotation.
      üîó
      üîó
    • We could define 1 unit to be a quarter rotation. Then, 4 units is a full rotation.
      üîó
      üîó
    Ultimately, units are a convention, and what units are useful or most natural depends on the context.
    üîó
    üîó
    With radians, the idea is that we will measure angles based on how long the arc of the angle is.
    üîó
    The longer the arc is, the bigger the angle that is swept out. The amount you rotate is proportional to how far you travel along the circle.
    üîó
    • Small rotation \(\rightarrow\) small distance traveled
      üîó
      üîó
    • Big rotation \(\rightarrow\) big distance traveled
      üîó
      üîó
    However, just measuring the arc doesn‚Äôt fully work, because the arc length depends on the size of the circle (basically, it depends on it‚Äôs radius).
    üîó
    • If you have a small circle, you won‚Äôt have to walk very far to make a full loop.
      üîó
      üîó
    • If you have a big circle, the same angle will mean you walk a lot farther.
      üîó
      üîó
    We want a measurement which purely measures rotation, that isn‚Äôt affected by how big the circle is. The key idea is: even though the arc length changes when the circle gets bigger or smaller, it changes in a very predictable way. In particular, arc length is proportional to the radius.
    üîó
    • If you double the radius, the arc length doubles.
      üîó
      üîó
    • If you triple the radius, the arc length triples.
      üîó
      üîó

    Example 9.1.8. Arc Length Proportionality.

    For example, for this particular angle \(\theta\) below, for \(r = 4\) cm, the arc is 8 cm, and for \(r = 8\) cm, the arc is 16 cm.
    üîó
    While the arc lengths are different, in both scenarios, the arc length is 2 radius lengths.
    üîó
    üîó
    To measure arc length, instead of using meters or centimeters or any other unit of length, we use the radius itself as the unit. In other words, we ask:
    üîó
    \begin{gather*} \text{``How many radius-lengths did you travel?"} \end{gather*}
    For example:
    üîó
    • If the radius is 4 m, and the arc length is 12 m, that is \(\frac{12}{4} = 3\) radius lengths of rotation.
      üîó
      üîó
    • If the radius is 3 m, and the arc length is 15 m, that is \(\frac{15}{3} = 5\) radius lengths of rotation.
      üîó
      üîó
    In other words, to measure the angle, divide the arc length by the radius length.
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection 9.1.4 Radians

    In short, radians measure angles based on how long the arc associated with the angle is, in terms of radius lengths.
    üîó

    Definition 9.1.9.

    One radian is defined to be the angle at the center of a circle such that the arc length associated with it is equal to the radius \(r\) of the circle,
    üîó
    More generally, an angle \(\theta\) with associated radius \(r\) and arc length \(s\) is given by,
    üîó
    \begin{gather*} \boxed{\theta = \frac{s}{r}} \end{gather*}
    üîó
    • In fancy math language, we often say that the angle \(\theta\) subtends the arc, or the arc is subtended by the angle \(\theta\text{.}\)
      üîó
      üîó
    • Radians are technically ‚Äúdimensionless‚Äù, because they are the ratio of two lengths.
      üîó
      üîó
