    Section 10.1 Intro to Proving Trigonometric Identities

    Proving a trigonometric identity involves algebraically manipulating one side of the equation, to show that both sides of the equation are equivalent.
    üîó

    Subsection 10.1.1 Verifying a Trigonometric Identity by Graphing

    A simple way to verify if an equation is an identity is to graph both sides using a graphing calculator, like Desmos.
    üîó
    If an equation is an identity, then both sides of the equation will be equal for all values of the variable, and so the graph of each side will coincide (i.e. overlap each other).
    üîó
    • If both graphs overlap completely, the equation is an identity.
      üîó
      üîó
    • On the other hand, if the two graphs don‚Äôt match at even a single point, then the equation is not an identity.
      üîó
      üîó
    With Desmo, it is pretty easy to tell if two graphs coincide. However, with a graphing calculator, minor differences can be difficult to spot.
    üîó
    üîó

    Example 10.1.1. Verification Example.

    Verify that \(1 + \cot^2{x} = \csc^2{x}\) using a graphing calculator. Observe how they coincide (overlap): Desmos graph.
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection 10.1.3 General Principles for Proving Identities

    • Convert all trigonometric expressions to sine and cosine. All trigonometric ratios can be written in terms of the ‚Äúbasic‚Äù trig ratios sine and cosine.
      üîó
      The one exception is if the entire identity includes only tangent and/or cotangent, in which case it is usually simpler to convert everything in terms of tangent.
      üîó
      üîó
    • Start by manipulating the more complicated side. This is because is generally easier to start from something complex and simplify it, rather than go from something simple and make it more complex. In general,
      • Tangent is more complicated than sine and cosine.
        üîó
        üîó
      • Reciprocal trig functions are more complicated than primary trig functions.
        üîó
        üîó
      • Multiple fractions are easier to combine into one fraction, compared to writing one fraction as a combination of multiple fractions.
        üîó
        üîó
      üîó
      üîó
    • Begin with the end in mind. Look at the form of the desired side before you start, and guide your work toward that structure. Especially helpful if you are stuck and unsure how to continue.
      üîó
      üîó
    • Combine fractions using a common denominator.
      üîó
      üîó
    • Multiply by the conjugate, in order to use the Pythagorean identity. Particularly if you see \(1 - \sin{x}, 1 + \sin{x}, 1 - \cos{x}, 1 + \cos{x}\text{.}\)
      üîó
      üîó
    • Simplify complex fractions, by clearing denominators (multiply the numerator and denominator by the LCD).
      üîó
      üîó
    • Use algebraic identities, e.g. difference of squares, square of a sum, etc.
      \begin{align*} a^2 - b^2 \amp = (a - b)(a + b) \end{align*}
      For example,
      • \(1 - \sin^2{x} = (1 - \sin{x})(1 + \sin{x})\text{.}\)
        üîó
        üîó
      • \(\sin^2{x} - \cos^2{x} = (\sin{x} - \cos{x})(\sin{x} + \cos{x})\text{.}\)
        üîó
        üîó
      üîó
      üîó
    • Use all trigonometric identities available to you. When practicing, use a list of them for reference, and you‚Äôll naturally memorize them over time.
      • Can convert \(\sin^2{x}\) to \(\cos^2{x}\) or vise versa, using the Pythagorean identity:
        \begin{align*} \sin^2{x} \amp = 1 - \cos^2{x}\\ \cos^2{x} \amp = 1 - \sin^2{x} \end{align*}
        üîó
        üîó
      üîó
      üîó
